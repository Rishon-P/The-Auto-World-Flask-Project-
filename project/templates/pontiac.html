Pontiac Motor Division

  • Pontiac, formally the Pontiac Motor Division of General Motors, was an American automobile brand owned, manufactured, and commercialized by General Motors.
  • It was introduced in 1926 as a companion make for GM's more expensive line of Oakland automobiles.
  • The Pontiac nameplate is derived from the town of Pontiac, Michigan, and a Native American chief of the same name .
  • In the hierarchy of GM's five divisions, it slotted above Chevrolet but below Oldsmobile, Buick, and Cadillac. Starting with the 1959 models, marketing was focused on selling the lifestyle that the car's ownership promised rather than the car itself.
  • Pontiac vehicles were actively involved in a variety of racing series, most notably NASCAR and the SCCA Trans-Am series. They also participated in USAC stock car racing and even made a name for themselves in drag racing. Pontiac also had a presence in other racing series, such as the Indianapolis 500, where they were used as pace cars.
  • Iconic Pontiac models include the GTO, Firebird, Grand Prix, and Trans Am, all known for their sporty handling and powerful engines.
  • Pontiac faced declining sales in the late 2000s as the market shifted towards more fuel-efficient vehicles and SUVs.
  • The General Motor's bankruptcy in 2009 led to the closure of the Pontiac brand, with the last car being built at the end of 2010.
  • Despite its demise, Pontiac remains a significant part of automotive history, known for its affordable performance cars and iconic models.
